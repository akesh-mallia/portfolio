High-Pressure Quick Disconnect

Wisconsin Space Program (WISP)

2026

Background

To fill our pressure-fed liquid rocket with gaseous nitrogen (GN2), the Wisconsin Space Program (WISP) needed a high-pressure (4,000 psi) fill line. Per competition rules, this fill line could not be remotely detached once the fill is complete. So, in Spring 2026, I owned a project for a remotely actuated quick disconnect for this fill line.

This was done via a system that siphoned some of the GN2 from the fill to pressurize a pneumatic cylinder that pushed out an arm, releasing the disconnect socket.

Challenges

Seperation Failure

The major motivator of the design process was that if this system failed, the rocket could not launch, as it would be stuck to the ground. My heart dropped during the test campaign when I experienced a separation failure of the system in April 2026 cold flows, just a month before the slated launch of the rocket. At first, the failure was confusing. However, through attempts at recreating the failure, I pinpointed the cause to the fill line not being vented. When pressurized, the bearings in the socket of the quick disconnect were pushed onto the plug, causing sufficient force to prevent detachment. After diagnosing the issue, the system had a 100% success rate.

Sizing Error

I first designed the system in isolation. This meant that I got a system that worked well when detaching from a rocket without an airframe. However, once the airframe was added, I noticed a crucial sizing error. The travel of the pneumatic piston arm was obstructed by the hole cut out in the airframe. Further, the vehicle side of the quick disconnect poked out of the diameter of the rocket. I did two things: to shorten the disconnect, I rearranged fluid fittings to remove an adapter, and I 3D printed a spacer to fit on the piston arm, allowing the travel of the arm to occur further away from the airframe.

Results

Separation Success!

On May 30, 2026, the disconnect separated, allowing for the launch of Northstar. This makes for a 100% success rate during flight scenarios :)

Pneumatic quick disconnect

The device and its parts are now in-house, with application to future launch vehicles.

Overall, I learned a lot about the design cycle in aerospace through this project. By designing, testing, and verifying the device, I gained experience in common struggles of a project: most notably, neglecting to consider other subsystems when designing. It was incredibly satisfying to see my part actuating and leading to the success of the mission.
